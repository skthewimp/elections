# May 2026 assembly results: the shape of the contest

I went into the May 2026 assembly results expecting the usual thing - seat counts, vote shares, a few neat headlines, and then some predictable overreading. What turned out to be more interesting was not who won, but how the contests themselves changed.

Tamil Nadu is the clearest example. In 2021, it still had 105 straight fights. In 2026, it has 2. That is not a small statistical quirk. That is the election telling you that the whole structure of competition has shifted. The state is no longer a simple two-pole map with a few noisy edges. It is much more crowded now, with TVK, DMK-led, AIADMK-led, and smaller forces all pulling the map in different directions. The regional footprint is also quite clear - Chennai, Kongu, Delta, South. This was not one statewide wave. It was several local movements that added up to something bigger.

Assam and West Bengal look more decisive on the surface, but the interesting part is that both are becoming cleaner binary contests, just in different ways. Assam ends up with the NDA at 102 of 126 seats and 48.0% of the vote. Bengal gives the BJP 206 of 294 seats and 45.8% of the vote. Those are not close results. But if you look at the geography of the swing, the story gets better. Assam’s movement is concentrated in places like Barak Valley, Lower Assam, Upper Assam, and the north. Bengal’s movement is split across North Bengal, Kolkata, Rarh, Jungle Mahal, and South Bengal. This was not a vague mood shift. It was regionally specific.

Kerala is the election that reminds you not every result needs to be dramatic to be interesting. It is still a proper UDF versus LDF fight, and the UDF’s jump in vote share is real. But the LDF is not vanishing anywhere. The seat margins stay competitive, and the swing is uneven enough that you can see the state moving without pretending it has collapsed. That matters. Kerala remains one of those states where the structure of contest is more useful than the headline seat count.

Puducherry is the messy one. NDA is ahead on seats, but only just, and the field is fragmented enough that the assembly still feels unstable. This is the kind of result where the difference between leading and controlling is large. The contest corners are small, the margins are tight, and the whole thing looks more like a series of local fights than a clean statewide verdict.

If I had to reduce the batch to one line, I would say this: Assam and Bengal are getting more bipolar, Kerala is still a genuine two-bloc contest, Puducherry remains fragmented, and Tamil Nadu has become much more multipolar than it was before. That is usually where the real story sits. Not in the winner column, but in the shape of the fight.
